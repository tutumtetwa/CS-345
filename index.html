Definition and History of Electronic and Internet Voting

Electronic and Internet voting refer to systems where technology is used to cast, transmit, and count votes. This includes electronic machines at polling stations as well as remote voting over the Internet.

Advantages 

Electronic voting enhances speed, accessibility, and accuracy, reshaping how modern democracies conduct elections.

Advantages of Electronic and Internet Voting

Faster Vote Counting

Automated systems quickly tabulate results, reducing delays and manual errors.

Convenience for Voters

People can vote easily from home or on mobile devices, increasing participation.

Reduced Human Error

Electronic systems minimize mistakes made during manual vote counting.

Accessibility for Remote Areas

Remote and disabled voters can participate without needing to travel to polling stations.

Improved Voter Turnout

Online voting options can boost participation by making voting easier and faster.

Environmentally Friendly

Less paper usage means fewer resources wasted and a lower environmental footprint.

Disadvantages 

Despite their convenience, electronic and online voting systems raise concerns about security, transparency, and equal access for all voters.

Security Risks and Cyber Attacks

Electronic voting systems are vulnerable to hacking, malware, and data breaches.

Threats to Voter Privacy

Online systems can potentially compromise voter anonymity and data protection.

Technical Failures and Software Bugs

Glitches, software errors, and system crashes can disrupt voting processes.

Digital Divide and Accessibility Issues

Not all voters have equal access to reliable internet or digital devices.

Lack of Transparency in Vote Counting

Electronic systems can make it harder to verify and audit election results.

Risk of Voter Disenfranchisement

Technical errors or cyberattacks could prevent voters from successfully casting ballots.
